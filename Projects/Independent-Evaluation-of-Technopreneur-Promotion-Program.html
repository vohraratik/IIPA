Project Overview

In 1998, the Government of India initiated the TePP Scheme as a joint effort between the DSIR's Programme Aimed at Technological Self Reliance (PATSER) Scheme and the Department of Science and Technology's (DST) Home Grown Technology (HGT) Programme of Technology Information Forecasting and Assessment Council (TIFAC). This scheme aimed to promote innovation and entrepreneurship among all sections of Indian society. TePP Outreach Centers (TUCs) were established throughout the country to attract talented individuals and facilitate the conversion of their innovations into viable commercial ventures. Indian Institute of Public Administration (IIPA) conducted a study that revealed the success of the TePP program. To publicize and promote the TUCs, marketing strategies such as print media, seminars, conferences, radio, national events, innovation camps, NGOs, Entrepreneurship Development Programs (EDPs), Faculty Development Programs (FDPs), and internet were employed by TUC coordinators. The majority of the innovators and entrepreneurs in Phase I and Phase II belonged to the first generation. There was a greater interest in innovation among the current generation than in previous generations. In Phase I, product innovations constituted 96.7% of the innovations, while in Phase II, the percentage was 95%. Healthcare, mechanical, and biotechnology were the areas of focus in Phase I. Phase II saw an increase in healthcare innovations (30%) and a reduction in biotechnology innovations (10.5%). The impact of the TePP Scheme was found to have improved the quality of life for many people by introducing innovative techniques in various areas, including oriental remedies, artificial cell culture manufacturing, and skin treatment plants.

Objectives

  • Identify and support innovative projects with commercial potential
  • Provide financial assistance to innovators for developing and commercializing their products
  • Encourage and promote entrepreneurship in the country
  • Facilitate the transfer of technology from academia and R&D institutions to the industry
  • Create a pool of successful technopreneurs who can mentor and guide others

Methodology

The evaluation used a mixed-methods approach, combining quantitative and qualitative methods. The quantitative data were collected through a survey of TePP-supported projects, while the qualitative data were collected through interviews with key stakeholders, including TePP officials, entrepreneurs, and experts in the field of technopreneurship.
The survey collected data on the profile of the TePP-supported projects, their achievements, and the impact of the TePP programme on their development. The survey also collected data on the challenges faced by the projects and their suggestions for improving the programme's design and implementation.
The qualitative data were collected through semi-structured interviews with key stakeholders. The interviews aimed to gather in-depth information on the stakeholders' perspectives on the TePP programme, its strengths and weaknesses, and suggestions for improvement.

The evaluation found that TePP had made a significant contribution to promoting innovation-based entrepreneurship in the country. Some of the key outcomes of the programme were:

  • Identification and support of innovative projects: TePP had supported over 3,000 projects since its launch, with a success rate of 45%.
  • Creation of a pool of successful technopreneurs: TePP had created a pool of successful technopreneurs who had commercialized their products and services and were now mentoring and guiding others.
  • Facilitation of technology transfer: TePP had facilitated the transfer of technology from academia and R&D institutions to the industry, leading to the development of new products and services.
  • Encouragement and promotion of entrepreneurship: TePP had played a significant role in encouraging and promoting entrepreneurship in the country, particularly among individuals and small-scale industries.

Conclusion

The Technopreneur Promotion Programme (TePP) is a unique scheme that supports innovators in India without requiring collateral security. Since its inception, the scheme has fostered an innovation and entrepreneurship culture in the country. However, there is a need to modify the existing process to make the scheme more successful and sustainable in the long run. To achieve this, the scheme should become more innovator-centric and provide maximum autonomy to entrepreneurs to generate enthusiasm among individuals. The existing network with industries can be explored for a market-driven approach to strengthen the linkage between industry and innovation.
To promote an entrepreneurial approach, the scheme must reduce the processing time for various operations, including fund disbursement, to reduce project costs, prevent technology obsolescence, and keep innovators motivated. The views of industry experts should be considered when disbursing funds, as the paucity of funds has led to delays. There is an immediate need to increase the financial resources allocated to the scheme. The system should be flexible and not generalized, as every innovation is unique.
The scheme can emphasize monitoring rather than evaluation for better impact, and the fund-related issues can be simplified by smoothening the internal processes to enhance beneficiaries' satisfaction levels. Provision of technical and non-technical skills training to beneficiaries would encourage their involvement. Experts from the industry should be included in the screening committee to create a market-driven flare for innovation. This linkage would help in generating self-employment opportunities and foster industry-academia partnerships.
The visibility of the scheme can be increased by creating awareness at the regional and national levels through in-depth promotional sessions and targeting academic institutes and media. Since Technical Up-gradation Centres (TUCs) play a significant role in the scheme's success, the selection of TUCs should be done judiciously. Institutions having innovation incubator cells like IIM, IITs, and NID should be given priority during TUC selection, or their model should be replicated to make the scheme more successful. To increase the accountability of the TUCs, a continuous follow-up of TUC coordinators is recommended to improve the monitoring system, leading to a reduction in the number of unsuccessful projects and an increased conversion rate. The Indian Institute of Public Administration recommends the continuation of the TePP scheme.
